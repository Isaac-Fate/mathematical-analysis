Reduction from Riemann-Stieltjes Integrals to Reimann Integrals#

Theorem 39

If \(f \in\mathfrak{R}(\alpha)\) on \([a, b]\), and \(\alpha\) is continuously differentiable on \([a, b]\), i.e., \(\alpha^\prime(x)\) exists for every \(x \in[a, b]\) and \(\alpha^\prime\) is continuous on \([a, b]\), then \(f \alpha^\prime\in\mathfrak{R}\), and

(62)#\[\begin{align}\int_a^b f \;\mathrm{d}\alpha = \int_a^b f(x) \alpha^\prime(x) \;\mathrm{d}x \end{align}\]

Proof. Choose an arbitrarily positive number \(\varepsilon > 0\). Since \(f \in\mathfrak{R}(\alpha)\), there exists a partition \(P_\varepsilon\) such that

(63)#\[\begin{align}\abs{S(P, f, \alpha) - \int_a^b f \; \mathrm{d}\alpha} < \frac{\varepsilon}{2}\quad\forall P \supset P_\varepsilon\end{align}\]

On the other hand, because \(\alpha^\prime\) is continuous, actually uniformly continuous on \([a, b]\), there exist \(\delta > 0\) such that

(64)#\[\begin{align}\abs{s - t} < \delta\implies\abs{\alpha^\prime(s) - \alpha^\prime(t)} < \frac{\varepsilon}{2M(b-a)}\end{align}\]

where \(M\) is a constant positive number such that \(\abs{f(x)} < M \;\forall x \in[a, b]\). Let \(P^\prime_\varepsilon\supset P_\varepsilon\) be a refinement such that the length of each subinterval is bounded by \(\delta\), i.e., \(\Delta x_k < \delta\).

Let \(P \supset P^\prime_\varepsilon\). We compare the Riemann sum \(S(P, f \alpha^\prime)\) to \(\int_a^b f \;\mathrm{d}\alpha\):

(65)#\[\begin{align}\abs{ S(P, f \alpha^\prime) - \int_a^b f \; \mathrm{d}\alpha }&\leq\abs{ S(P, f \alpha^\prime) - S(P, f, \alpha) } + \abs{ S(P, f, \alpha) - \int_a^b f \; \mathrm{d}\alpha }\end{align}\]

where \(S(P, f \alpha^\prime)\) and \(S(P, f, \alpha)\) share the same choice of point \(t_k\) in each subinterval \([x_{k-1}, x_k]\). We consider each term on the right-hand side of (65) in the following.

For the term \(\abs{ S(P, f \alpha^\prime) - S(P, f, \alpha) }\), we have

\[\begin{split}\begin{align*}\abs{ S(P, f \alpha^\prime) - S(P, f, \alpha) }&= \abs{ \sum \left ( f(t_k) \alpha^\prime(t_k) \Delta x_k - f(t_k) \Delta \alpha_k \right ) }\\&= \abs{ \sum f(t_k) \left ( \alpha^\prime(t_k) - \alpha^\prime(\xi_k) \right ) \Delta x_k }\quad\text{by the Mean Value Theorem}\\&\leq\sum\abs{f(t_k)}\abs{ \alpha^\prime(t_k) - \alpha^\prime(\xi_k) }\Delta x_k \\&\leq M \sum\abs{ \alpha^\prime(t_k) - \alpha^\prime(\xi_k) }\Delta x_k \quad\text{recall $\abs{f} < M$}\\&< M \frac{\varepsilon}{2M(b-a)}\sum\Delta x_k \\&= \frac{\varepsilon}{2}\end{align*}\end{split}\]

Note that the second last inequality follows from (64) since \(\abs{t_k - \xi_k} < \delta\). In summary, we have obtained

(66)#\[\begin{align}\abs{ S(P, f \alpha^\prime) - S(P, f, \alpha) } < \frac{\varepsilon}{2}\end{align}\]

We also need to consider the term \(\abs{S(P, f, \alpha) - \int_a^b f \; \mathrm{d}\alpha}\) in (65). Recall that \(P \supset P_\varepsilon\). It then follows from (63) that

(67)#\[\begin{align}\abs{ S(P, f, \alpha) - \int_a^b f \; \mathrm{d}\alpha } < \frac{\varepsilon}{2}\end{align}\]

Finally, taking into consideration (65), (66) and (67), we obtain

\[\begin{align*}\abs{ S(P, f \alpha^\prime) - \int_a^b f \; \mathrm{d}\alpha } < \frac{\varepsilon}{2} + \frac{\varepsilon}{2} = \varepsilon\quad\forall P \supset P^\prime_\varepsilon\end{align*}\]

This implies that \(f\alpha^\prime\in\mathfrak{R}\), and (62) indeed holds.

Theorem 40

Suppose \(f, g \in\mathfrak{R}\) on \([a, b]\). Let functions \(F, G\) on \([a, b]\) be defined by

\[\begin{align*} F(x) &:= \int_a^x f(t) \;\mathrm{d}t & G(x) &:= \int_a^x g(t) \;\mathrm{d}t \end{align*}\]

Then \(F\) and \(G\) are both continuous and of bounded variation on \([a, b]\). Moreover, \(f \in\mathfrak{R}(G)\) and \(g \in\mathfrak{R}(F)\). In this case,

(68)#\[\begin{align}\int_a^b f(x) g(x) \;\mathrm{d}x = \int_a^b f \;\mathrm{d}G = \int_a^b g \;\mathrm{d}F \end{align}\]

Proof. By Theorem 34, we know \(F\) and \(G\) are of bounded variation on \([a, b]\). In addition, since identity integrator \(t\) is clearly continuous on \([a, b]\), it follows that \(F\) and \(G\) are also continuous.

Then, by Theorem 29, we have \(f \in\mathfrak{R}(G)\) and \(g \in g \in\mathfrak{R}(F)\), and the equality (68) holds.

Theorem 41

Suppose \(f \in\mathfrak{R}\) on \([a, b]\). If function \(F\) is defined on \((a, b)\) with

\[\begin{align*} F^\prime(x) = f(x) \quad\forall x \in(a, b) \end{align*}\]

Moreover, suppose \(F(a+)\) and \(F(b-)\) both exist as finite values. Then we have

(69)#\[\begin{align}\int_a^b f(x) \;\mathrm{d}x = F(b-) - F(a+) \end{align}\]

Proof. (Extension of \(F\)) We first extend the function \(F\) to the entire closed interval \([a, b]\) by defining

\[\begin{align*} F(a) &:= F(a+) & F(b) &:= F(b-) \end{align*}\]

(Applying Mean Value Theorem) For a partition \(P\) on \([a, b]\), we have

\[\begin{align*} F(b-) - F(a+) = F(b) - F(a) = \sum_k F(x_k) - F(x_{k-1}) \end{align*}\]

Then, by applying the extended version of Generalized Mean Value Theorem (Theorem 13), there exists \(t_k \in(x_k, x_{k-1})\) such that

\[\begin{align*} F(x_k) - F(x_{k-1}) = f(t_k) \Delta x_k \end{align*}\]

It then follows that

(70)#\[\begin{align} F(b-) - F(a+) = \sum_k f(t_k) \Delta x_k = S(P, f) \end{align}\]

(Applying Integrability) Given \(\varepsilon > 0\), since \(f \in\mathfrak{R}\) on \([a, b]\), there exists a partition \(P_\varepsilon\) such that for any refinement \(P \supset P_\varepsilon\), we have

(71)#\[\begin{align}\abs{S(P, f) - \int_a^b f \; \mathrm{d}x} < \varepsilon\quad\forall P \supset P_\varepsilon\end{align}\]

Equation (69) then follows from (70) and (71).

Example 6

Consider function

\[\begin{align*} F(x) = \frac{x \sin(1 - x^2)}{1 - x^2}\quad x \in(-1, 1) \end{align*}\]

The derivative \(f\) of \(F\) exists on \((-1, 1)\). After some computation, we will obtain the following expression for \(f\):

\[\begin{align*} f(x) = F^\prime(x) = \frac{(1+x^2)\sin(1-x^2) - 2x^2(1-x^2)\cos(1-x^2)}{(1-x^2)^2}\quad x \in(-1, 1) \end{align*}\]

Note that \(f\) is clearly continuous. Though \(f\) is defined in \((-1, 1)\), the one-sided limits at two endpoints both exist, and

(72)#\[\begin{align} f(-1+) = f(1-) = 1 \end{align}\]

The above result (72) is not that obvious, and is left as an exercise (✍️). Hence, we can extend \(f\) on \([-1, 1]\) by defining

\[\begin{align*} f(-1) &:= 1 & f(1) &:= 1 \end{align*}\]

and \(f\) remains continuous on \([-1, 1]\). The plotting of \(f\) and \(F\) is illustrated in Fig. 1.

Now, we want to compute the integral of \(f\) on \([-1, 1]\), i.e., \(\int_{-1}^1 f(x) \;\mathrm{d}x\). Applying Theorem 41, we have

\[\begin{align*}\int_{-1}^1 f(x) \;\mathrm{d}x = F(1-) - F(-1+) = 1 - (-1) =2 \end{align*}\]

Theorem 42

Suppose \(f \in\mathfrak{R}\) and \(\alpha\) is continuous on \([a, b]\). Suppose further \(\alpha^\prime\) exists and \(\alpha^\prime\in\mathfrak{R}\) on \([a, b]\). Then \(f \in\mathfrak{R}(\alpha)\) and \(f \alpha^\prime\in\mathfrak{R}\) on \([a, b]\). In this case,

(73)#\[\begin{align}\int_a^b f(x) \;\mathrm{d}\alpha(x) = \int_a^b f(x) \alpha^\prime(x) \;\mathrm{d}x \end{align}\]

Remark 12

Note that the requirement of \(\alpha\) being continuous is actually redundant since we assume that \(\alpha^\prime\) exists on \([a, b]\).

Proof. Since \(f, \alpha^\prime\in\mathfrak{R}\), it follows from Theorem 28 that their product is also integrable, i.e., \(f \alpha^\prime\in\mathfrak{R}\). Theorem 40 tells us we may put \(\alpha^\prime\) into the integrator in the sense that

(74)#\[\begin{align}\int_a^b f(x) \alpha^\prime(x) \;\mathrm{d}x = \int_a^b f(x) \;\mathrm{d} A(x) \end{align}\]

where

\[\begin{align*} A(x) = \int_a^x \alpha^\prime(t) \;\mathrm{d}t \end{align*}\]

But we know from Theorem 41 that

\[\begin{align*}\alpha(x) - \alpha(a) = \int_a^x \alpha^\prime(t) \;\mathrm{d}t \end{align*}\]

which implies

\[\begin{align*} A(x) = \alpha(x) - \alpha(a) \end{align*}\]

Then (74) reduces to

(75)#\[\begin{align}\int_a^b f(x) \alpha^\prime(x) \;\mathrm{d}x = \int_a^b f(x) \;\mathrm{d}(\alpha(x) - \alpha(a)) \end{align}\]

Note that \(\alpha(a)\) is just a constant, and hence \(f \in\mathfrak{R}(\alpha(a))\) and \(\int_a^b f (x) \;\mathrm{d}\alpha(a) = 0\). Due to the linear property (Theorem 20) of the integrals, we have

(76)#\[\begin{align}\int_a^b f(x) \;\mathrm{d}(\alpha(x) - \alpha(a)) = \int_a^b f(x) \;\mathrm{d}\alpha(x) - \int_a^b f(x) \;\mathrm{d}\alpha(a) = \int_a^b f(x) \;\mathrm{d}\alpha(x) \end{align}\]

Finally, (73) follows from (75) and (76).
