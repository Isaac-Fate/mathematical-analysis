Power Series#

In this section, we study the properties of power series, i.e., the function of the form

\[\begin{align*} f(x) = \sum c_n x^n \end{align*}\]

where \(c_n, x \in\R\). The reason why we confine ourselves to real values is that we have only defined differentiation and integration in the real field.

Theorem 42

Suppose that the power series

\[\begin{align*}\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} c_n x^n \end{align*}\]

converges for \(\abs{x} < R\) where \(c_n, x \in\R\) and \(R > 0\). Define

\[\begin{align*} f(x) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} c_n x^n \end{align*}\]

Then \(f\) converges uniformly on \([-R + \varepsilon, R - \varepsilon]\) for any \(\varepsilon\in(0, R)\). Moreover, \(f\) is differentiable in the interval \((-R, R)\), and

\[\begin{align*} f^\prime(x) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} n c_n x^{n-1}\end{align*}\]

where \(k \in\N\)(\(f^{(0)}\) means \(f\)). Furthermore, putting \(x = 0\) in (48), we obtain the equality

(49)#\[\begin{align} c_n = \frac{f^{(n)}(0)}{n!}\end{align}\]

Negative Binomial Theorem#

It is well known that

\[\begin{align*}\frac{1}{1-x} = \sum_{k=0}^\infty x^k \quad -1 < x < 1 \end{align*}\]

The right-hand side is a geometric series, and we can easily compute its explicit value, which equals the left-hand side. The Negative Binomial Theorem is a generalization for it states the power series representation of the function \(\frac{1}{(1-x)^n}\).

Theorem 43

We have the following identity:

(50)#\[\begin{align}\frac{1}{(1-x)^n} = \sum_{k=0}^\infty\binom{n+k-1}{k} x^k \end{align}\]

where \(x \in\R\), \(\abs{x} < 1\) and \(n \in\Ns\). Moreover, the power series on right-hand side of (50) converges only when \(\abs{x} < 1\).

Proof. Let

\[\begin{align*} f(x) = \frac{1}{1-x}\quad -1 < x < 1 \end{align*}\]

The \((n-1)\)-th order of derivative of \(f\) is

(51)#\[\begin{align} f^{(n-1)}(x) = \frac{(n-1)!}{(1-x)^{n}}\end{align}\]

On the other hand, applying (48) in Corollary 2, we obtain

(52)#\[\begin{align} f^{(n-1)}(x) = \sum_{k=n-1}^\infty k(k-1) \cdots(k-n+1) x^{k-n} = \sum_{k=0}^\infty(k+n-1)(k+n-2) \cdots(k+1) x^{k}\end{align}\]

Comparing (51) and (52), we have

\[\begin{align*}\frac{(n-1)!}{(1-x)^{n}} = \sum_{k=0}^\infty(k+n-1)(k+n-2) \cdots(k+1) x^{k}\end{align*}\]

Therefore,

\[\begin{align*}\frac{1}{(1-x)^{n}} = \sum_{k=0}^\infty\frac{(k+n-1)(k+n-2) \cdots (k+1)}{(n-1)!} x^{k} = \sum_{k=0}^\infty\binom{n+k-1}{k} x^{k}\end{align*}\]

So far, we have shown (50) indeed converges in \(\abs{x} < 1\). We also need to show that it cannot converge for \(\abs{x}\geq 1\). In fact, we only need to show it does not converge for \(\abs{x} = 1\) due to Theorem 10. Suppose \(\abs{x} = 1\). We consider the absolute value of each term of the series.

(53)#\[\begin{align}\abs{\binom{n+k-1}{k} x^k} = \binom{n+k-1}{k} = \binom{n+k-1}{n-1}\end{align}\]

Note that the combinatorial number (53) will tend to \(\infty\) as \(k \to\infty\). Thus, series (50) will of course diverge for \(\abs{x} = 1\).

We note that \(\frac{1}{(1-x)^n}\) is obtained by multiplying \(\frac{1}{1-x}\) by itself \(n-1\) times. It is tempting to multiply the power series \(\sum x^k\). Hence, another strategy for proving this theorem is to compute the Cauchy Product of these \(n\) series \(\sum x^k\).

In the alternative proof, we will need an identity from combinatorial mathematics, which is known as the Hockey-Stick Identity.

Theorem 44

We have the identity:

\[\begin{align*}\sum_{i=r}^n \binom{i}{r} = \binom{n+1}{r+1}\end{align*}\]

We now provide an alternative proof of the Negative Binomial Theorem as follows.

Proof. (Proof of Theorem 43 using Cauchy Product) We shall prove by induction on \(n\).

(Base Case) If \(n = 1\), then (50) is

\[\begin{align*}\frac{1}{1-x} = \sum_{k=0}^\infty x^k \end{align*}\]

which is already known.

(Inductive Step) Assume (50) holds for \(n = m\), we shall prove it also holds for \(n = m+1\). We have

\[\begin{split}\begin{align*}\frac{1}{1-x}&= \sum_{k=0}^\infty x^k \\\frac{1}{(1-x)^m}&= \sum_{k=0}^\infty\binom{m+k-1}{k} x^k \end{align*}\end{split}\]

Note that \(\sum x^k\) converges absolutely for \(\abs{x} < 1\). Then by Theorem 12, the Cauchy Product of two series on the right-hand sides converges, and

(54)#\[\begin{align}\sum_{k=0}^\infty\sum_{j=0}^k x^{k-j}\binom{m+j-1}{j} x^j = \sum_{k=0}^\infty x^k \sum_{k=0}^\infty\binom{m+k-1}{k} x^k = \frac{1}{1-x}\frac{1}{(1-x)^m} = \frac{1}{(1-x)^{m+1}}\end{align}\]

We now compute the Cauchy product on the left-hand side of (54).

\[\begin{split}\begin{align*}\sum_{k=0}^\infty\sum_{j=0}^k x^{k-j}\binom{m+j-1}{j} x^j &= \sum_{k=0}^\infty\sum_{j=0}^k \binom{m+j-1}{j} x^k \\&= \sum_{k=0}^\infty\sum_{j=0}^k \binom{m+j-1}{m-1} x^k \\&= \sum_{k=0}^\infty\sum_{i=m-1}^{m+k-1}\binom{i}{m-1} x^k \\&= \sum_{k=0}^\infty\binom{m+k}{m} x^k \quad\text{by \ref{thm:16}}\\&= \sum_{k=0}^\infty\binom{m+k}{k} x^k \end{align*}\end{split}\]

Hence, the Cauchy product equals

(55)#\[\begin{align}\sum_{k=0}^\infty\sum_{j=0}^k x^{k-j}\binom{m+j-1}{j} x^j = \sum_{k=0}^\infty\binom{m+k}{k} x^k \end{align}\]

It then follows from (54) and (55) that

\[\begin{align*}\frac{1}{(1-x)^{m+1}} = \sum_{k=0}^\infty\binom{m+k}{k} x^k \end{align*}\]

which is exactly (50) with \(n=m+1\).

Taylorâ€™s Theorem#

The Taylorâ€™s Theorem states that we can expand a power series as a Taylor series about some point in the interval of convergence.

Theorem 45

Suppose power series

\[\begin{align*} f(x) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty c_n x^n \end{align*}\]

converges in \(\abs{x} < R\)(\(R > 0\)). Let \(a \in(-R, R)\), then \(f(x)\) can be expanded in a power series about point \(a\):

(56)#\[\begin{align} f(x) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty\frac{f^{(n)}(a)}{n!}(x-a)^n \end{align}\]

which converges in \(\abs{x-a} < R - \abs{a}\).

Remark 20

Note that the radius of convergence of the power series (56) is at least\(R - \abs{a}\)(provided that \(R\) is the radius of convergence of \(\sum c_n x^n\)). It may converge in a larger interval about point \(a\). Having said that, there also exists a case that \(R - \abs{a}\) is precisely the radius of convergence of (56). We will illustrate this in Example 9.

Proof. To obtain the term \((x-a)\), we apply the Binomial Theorem:

(57)#\[\begin{align} f(x) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty c_n x^n = \sum_{n=0}^\infty c_n \left(a + (x-a)\right)^n = \sum_{n=0}^\infty\sum_{k=0}^n c_n \binom{n}{k} a^{n-k}(x-a)^k \end{align}\]

Define

\[\begin{split}\begin{align*} p_{nk} := \begin{cases} c_n \binom{n}{k} a^{n-k} (x-a)^k &k \leq n \\ 0 &k > n \end{cases}\end{align*}\end{split}\]

Then, we can rewrite (57) as

(58)#\[\begin{align} f(x) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty\sum_{k=0}^\infty p_{nk}\end{align}\]

We intend to apply Theorem 11 to interchange the order of summations in (58). To do so, we need to verify

  1. \(\sum_{k=0}^\infty\abs{p_{nk}} =: b_n < \infty\)

  2. \(\sum_{n=0}^\infty b_n < \infty\)

(Checking \(\sum_{k=0}^\infty\abs{p_{nk}} =: b_n < \infty\)) We have

\[\begin{split}\begin{align*} b_n = \sum_{k=0}^\infty\abs{p_{nk}}&= \sum_{k=0}^n \abs{p_{nk}}\\&= \sum_{k=0}^n \abs{c_n}\binom{n}{k}\abs{a}^{n-k}\abs{x-a}^k \\&= \abs{c_n}\left(\abs{a} + \abs{x-a}\right)^n \end{align*}\end{split}\]

Therefore,

(59)#\[\begin{align} b_n = \abs{c_n}\left(\abs{a} + \abs{x-a}\right)^n \end{align}\]

is indeed a finite number.

(Checking \(\sum_{n=0}^\infty b_n < \infty\)) Suppose that \(\abs{x-a} < R - \abs{a}\). Then there exists some \(\varepsilon > 0\) such that

\[\begin{align*}\abs{a} + \abs{x-a} = R - \varepsilon\end{align*}\]

Applying (59), \(b_n\) is bounded above by

\[\begin{align*} b_n = \abs{c_n}\left(\abs{a} + \abs{x-a}\right)^n < \abs{c_n}\left(R - \varepsilon\right)^n \end{align*}\]

Note that Theorem 42 implies that \(\sum c_n (R-\varepsilon)^n\) converges absolutely, i.e., \(\sum\abs{c_n}\left(R - \varepsilon\right)^n\) converges. It follows that \(\sum b_n\) also converges by the Comparison Test.

Indeed, we are allowed to change the order of summations in (58). It then follows that

\[\begin{split}\begin{align*} f(x) &= \sum_{n=0}^\infty\sum_{k=0}^\infty p_{nk}\\&= \sum_{k=0}^\infty\sum_{n=0}^\infty p_{nk}\\&= \sum_{k=0}^\infty\sum_{n=k}^\infty c_n \binom{n}{k} a^{n-k}(x-a)^k \quad\text{recall definition of $p_{nk}$}\\&= \sum_{k=0}^\infty\frac{f^{(k)}(a)}{k!}(x-a)^k \quad\text{putting $x=a$ in \eqref{eq:33}}\end{align*}\end{split}\]

Example 9

Let

\[\begin{align*} f(x) = \frac{1}{1-x} = \sum_{n=0}^\infty x^n \quad -1 < x < 1 \end{align*}\]

The derivatives of \(f\) are

\[\begin{align*} f^{(n)}(x) = \frac{n!}{(1-x)^{n+1}}\end{align*}\]

We now expand \(f(x)\) about some point \(a \in(-1, 1)\) using Theorem 45

(60)#\[\begin{align} f(x) = \frac{1}{1-x} = \sum_{n=0}^\infty\frac{(x-a)^n}{(1-a)^{n+1}}\end{align}\]

The radius of convergence of power series (60) can be computed in two ways. The first way is by the Root Test.

\[\begin{align*}\limsup_{n \to \infty}\frac{1}{\sqrt[n]{\abs{1-a}^{n+1}}} = \frac{1}{\abs{1-a}}\end{align*}\]

Hence, the radius of convergence is

\[\begin{align*} R = \frac{1}{\limsup_{n \to \infty} \frac{1}{\sqrt[n]{\abs{1-a}^{n+1}}}} = \abs{1-a}\end{align*}\]

On the other hand, observe that (60) is a geometric series. Therefore, it converges if and only if

\[\begin{align*}\abs{\frac{x-a}{1-a}} < 1 \end{align*}\]

That is, \(\abs{x-a} < \abs{1-a}\), which implies that the radius of convergence is \(\abs{1-a}\).

If \(a \geq 0\), then

\[\begin{align*} R = \abs{1-a} = 1 - \abs{a}\end{align*}\]

which means (60) only converges in the range that is stated in Theorem 45.

However, if \(a < 0\), then

\[\begin{align*} R = \abs{1-a} = 1 + \abs{a} > 1 - \abs{a}\end{align*}\]

Therefore, (60) may converge in a larger range.
