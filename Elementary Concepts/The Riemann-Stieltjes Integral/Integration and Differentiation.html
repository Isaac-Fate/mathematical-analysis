Integration and Differentiation#

Theorem 27

Suppose \(\alpha\) is of bounded variation on \([a, b]\), and \(f \in\mathfrak{R}(\alpha)\) on \([a, b]\). Define a function \(F\) on \([a, b]\) by

\[\begin{align*} F(x) := \int_a^x f \;\mathrm{d}\alpha\end{align*}\]

Then we have the following:

  1. \(F\) is of bounded variation on \([a, b]\).

  2. If \(\alpha\) is continuous at \(x_0\), then \(F\) is also continuous at \(x_0\).

  3. Suppose that \(\alpha\) is increasing. Then \(F\) is differentiable at some point \(x_0 \in(a, b)\) where \(\alpha\) is differentiable and \(f\) is continuous. That is, if \(\alpha^\prime(x_0)\) exists and \(f\) is continuous at \(x_0\), then \(F^\prime(x_0)\) exists. In that case,

\[\begin{align*} F^\prime(x_0) = f(x_0) \alpha^\prime(x_0) \end{align*}\]

The proof of this theorem is an application of the First Mean Value Theorem for Riemann-Stieltjes Integrals (Theorem 25), which we shall present in the following.

Proof. (Sufficiency of Only Considering the Case Where \(\alpha\) Is Increasing) Assume we can prove 1 and 2 for increasing \(\alpha\). Then by applying Theorem 17, Theorem 19 and Theorem 15, we can prove 1 and 2 for \(\alpha\) that is of bounded variation. Therefore, it suffices to only prove 1 and 2 assuming \(\alpha\) is increasing.

(Result from the First Mean Value Theorem) For any pair of points \(x_1, x_2 \in[a, b]\). By Theorem 25, there exists some value \(c\) such that

(37)#\[\begin{align} F(x_2) - F(x_1) = c (\alpha(x_2) - \alpha(x_1)) \end{align}\]

Note that (37) follows from Theorem 25 provided that \(x_1 < x_2\). But the cases where \(x_1 > x_2\) and \(x_1 = x_2\) will also hold immediately by a few steps of arguments. We also have the following inequality, which follows from (37):

(38)#\[\begin{align}\abs{F(x_2) - F(x_1)} = M \abs{\alpha(x_2) - \alpha(x_1)}\end{align}\]

where \(M\) is a constant such that \(M > \abs{f(x)}\;\forall x \in[a, b]\).

(Proof of 1) For any partition \(P = \left\{x_0, \ldots, x_n\right\}\) on \([a, b]\), by applying (38), we obtain

\[\begin{align*} V(P, F) = \sum\abs{F(x_i) - F(x_{i-1})}\leq M \sum\abs{\alpha(x_i) - \alpha(x_{i-1})} = M (b-a) \end{align*}\]

Note that the last equality follows from the assumption that \(\alpha\) is increasing. Therefore, \(V(P, F)\) is bounded above by a constant positive number, which implies \(F\) is indeed of bounded variation on \([a, b]\).

(Proof of 2) Given \(\varepsilon > 0\), since \(\alpha\) is continuous at \(x_0\), there exist \(\delta > 0\) such that

\[\begin{align*}\abs{x - x_0} < \delta\implies\abs{\alpha(x) - \alpha(x_0)} < \varepsilon / M \end{align*}\]

For \(x\) satisfying \(\abs{x - x_0} < \delta\), it then follows from (38) that

\[\begin{align*}\abs{F(x) - F(x_0)} = M \abs{\alpha(x) - \alpha(x_0)} < \varepsilon\end{align*}\]

This implies \(F\) is continuous at \(x_0\).

(Proof of 3) Let \(\varepsilon > 0\) be arbitrary. Because \(\alpha\) is differentiable at \(x_0\), there exists \(\delta_1 > 0\) such that

(39)#\[\begin{align}\abs{x - x_0} < \delta_1 \implies\abs{\frac{\alpha(x) - \alpha(x_0)}{x - x_0} - \alpha^\prime(x_0)} < \varepsilon\end{align}\]

And because \(f\) is continuous at \(x_0\), there exists \(\delta_2 > 0\) such that

(40)#\[\begin{align}\abs{x - x_0} < \delta_2 \implies\abs{f(x) - f(x_0)} < \varepsilon\end{align}\]

To ease the notation, let \(I_x\) denote the interval with endpoints \(x\) and \(x_0\). Let \(\delta = \min\left\{\delta_1, \delta_2\right\}\).

In the rest of the proof we consider point \(x\) satisfying \(\abs{x - x_0} < \delta\), it then follows from (37) that

(41)#\[\begin{align} F(x) - F(x_0) = c_x (\alpha(x) - \alpha(x_0)) \end{align}\]

where \(c_x\) depends on the choice of \(x\). Moreover,

\[\begin{align*}\inf_{t \in I_x} f(t) \leq c_x \leq\sup_{t\in I_x} f(t) \end{align*}\]

From (41), we have

(42)#\[\begin{align}\abs{ \frac{F(x) - F(x_0)}{x - x_0} - f(x_0) \alpha^\prime(x_0) }\leq\abs{ c_x }\abs{ \frac{\alpha(x) - \alpha(x_0)}{x - x_0} - \alpha^\prime(x_0) } + \abs{ \alpha^\prime(x_0) }\abs{ c_x - f(x_0) }\end{align}\]

Our goal is to bound the right-hand side of (42) by an arbitrarily small quantity.

Let us first consider expressions involving \(c_x\). It follows from (40) that

\[\begin{align*}\abs{\sup_{t \in I_x}f(t) - f(x_0)}&\leq\varepsilon& \abs{\inf_{t \in I_x}f(t) - f(x_0)}&\leq\varepsilon\end{align*}\]

which further implies

\[\begin{align*}\sup_{t \in I_x}f(t) - \inf_{t \in I_x}f(t) \leq 2\varepsilon\end{align*}\]

Since \(c_x\) and \(f(x_0)\) both take values in between \(\inf_{t \in I_x}f(t)\) and \(\sup_{t \in I_x}f(t)\), we have

(43)#\[\begin{align}\abs{c_x - f(x_0)}\leq 2\varepsilon\end{align}\]

And the value of \(c_x\) itself is bounded by

(44)#\[\begin{align}\abs{c_x}\leq\abs{f(x_0)} + 2\varepsilon\end{align}\]

Finally, by taking into consideration (42), (43), (44) and (39), we have

\[\begin{align*}\abs{x - x_0}\implies\abs{ \frac{F(x) - F(x_0)}{x - x_0} - f(x_0) \alpha^\prime(x_0) }\leq\left( \abs{f(x_0)} + 2\varepsilon\right) \varepsilon + \abs{\alpha^\prime(x_0)}\cdot 2\varepsilon\end{align*}\]

Observe that the right-hand side of the inequality above is indeed an arbitrarily small quantity. This completes the proof.

Theorem 28

Let \(f \in\mathfrak{R}\) on \([a, b]\). For \(x \in[a, b]\), put

\[\begin{align*} F(x) = \int_a^x f(t) \;\mathrm{d} t \end{align*}\]

Then, \(F\) is continuous on \([a, b]\)(more precisely, \(F\) is uniformly continuous). Furthermore, if \(f\) is continuous at some point \(x_0 \in[a, b]\), then \(F\) is differentiable at \(x_0\), and

\[\begin{align*} F^\prime(x_0) = f(x_0) \end{align*}\]

Example 4

Consider the function

\[\begin{split}\begin{align*} f(x) = \begin{cases} 2x \sin \frac{1}{x} - \cos \frac{1}{x} &-\frac{1}{\pi} \leq x \leq \frac{1}{\pi}, \; x \neq 0 \\ 0 &x = 0 \end{cases}\end{align*}\end{split}\]

Let \(F(x) = \int_{-1/\pi}^{x} f(t) \;\mathrm{d} t\), then a few steps of computation shows

\[\begin{split}\begin{align*} F(x) = \begin{cases} x^2 \sin \frac{1}{x} &-\frac{1}{\pi} \leq x \leq \frac{1}{\pi}, \; x \neq 0 \\ 0 &x = 0 \end{cases}\end{align*}\end{split}\]

Note that even though \(f\) is discontinuous at \(x = 0\), \(F\) remains continuous on the entire interval. But, because of this discontinuity of \(f\), \(F\) is not differentiable at \(x=0\).

Theorem 29 (Fundamental Theorem of Calculus)

If \( f \in\mathfrak{R}\) on \([a, b]\) and \(F\) is a differentiable function on \([a, b]\) such that \(F^\prime = f\), then we have

\[\begin{align*}\int_a^b f \;\mathrm{d} x = F(b) - F(a) \end{align*}\]
